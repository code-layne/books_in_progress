# Chapter 1 — *Circe* Journal Prompts

## I. Opening Voice & Narrative Authority

1. The novel opens not with grandeur, but with diminishment: Circe describes herself as “least” among divine beings. How does Miller establish narrative authority through apparent weakness or marginality? What is the effect of beginning from exclusion rather than power?

2. Examine the tension between immortality and insignificance in the opening pages. How does Circe’s social position complicate traditional assumptions about divinity?

3. Circe repeatedly draws attention to sound, voice, and speech. What role does vocality play in this chapter? Consider:
    - who is permitted to speak,
    - who is heard,
    - whose speech is dismissed,
    - and how Circe narrates her own voice.

4. What kind of narrator does Circe initially appear to be? Detached? Bitter? Observant? Naïve? Retrospective? How does Miller construct this voice stylistically?

---

## II. Divine Society & Social Hierarchy

5. Analyze the culture of the gods as presented in Chapter 1. What values govern divine society? Strength? Beauty? Cruelty? Performance? Utility?

6. To what extent does immortality appear emotionally deadening in this chapter? Identify moments where divine behavior seems emptied of moral or emotional weight.

7. Consider Helios carefully as a father and sovereign figure. How does Miller characterize power through him? Does his authority seem stable, performative, fearful, or something else entirely?

8. The gods often react to vulnerability with contempt. Why might vulnerability be especially dangerous in an immortal society?

---

## III. Physicality, Monstrosity, and Selfhood

9. Circe’s body and appearance are described in contrast to other divinities. How does physical description operate socially in this chapter? What assumptions are attached to beauty and power?

10. Examine moments where Circe internalizes the judgments of others. How does self-perception become socially constructed?

11. Is Circe truly “lesser,” or has she merely accepted a framework created by others? Explore the difference between essence and imposed identity.

---

## IV. Language & Style

12. Track Miller’s descriptive language regarding:
- light,
- gold,
- fire,
- animality,
- and sound.

What symbolic patterns begin emerging already in Chapter 1?

13. Miller adapts mythological material into modern prose without sounding archaic. Identify passages where the prose feels especially contemporary or psychologically intimate. Why do these stylistic choices matter?

14. How does the pacing of the chapter function? Where does Miller linger? Where does she compress? What do those choices suggest about what the narrator values?

---

## V. Thematic Inquiry

15. Chapter 1 is deeply concerned with hierarchy. Identify at least three distinct hierarchies operating simultaneously in the text:
- divine/mortal,
- male/female,
- beautiful/ugly,
- powerful/powerless,
- voiced/silenced,
- etc.

How do these systems reinforce one another?

16. To what degree is exile already psychologically present before literal exile occurs in the novel?

17. What does the chapter suggest about the relationship between observation and alienation? Does Circe become perceptive because she is excluded?

---

## VI. Advanced Critical Questions

18. Many classical epics focus on heroic action. *Circe* instead foregrounds perception, memory, and interiority. What does this shift accomplish politically or philosophically?

19. Consider whether this chapter functions as a critique of aristocratic or patriarchal systems broadly—not merely divine mythology. Where might the novel invite modern parallels?

20. What is the significance of beginning a mythological retelling from the perspective of a figure traditionally sidelined in canonical texts such as *The Odyssey*?

# Stylistic Observations to Track Throughout the Novel

As you read, begin keeping notes on recurring motifs:

- transformation
- hunger
- voice/silence
- animal imagery
- craftsmanship
- loneliness
- naming
- power through observation
- predation
- shame
- fire/light

You should especially watch how Miller links emotional states to physical and natural imagery.

# Guidance for Your Responses

When you write:
- avoid merely answering every question sequentially;
- instead, pursue 2–4 questions deeply and organically;
- build an argument rather than a worksheet response;
- quote selectively and analyze closely.

A shorter, incisive analysis is far stronger than a long summary.

When you are ready, submit your reflections for critique focused on:
- analytical rigor,
- interpretive depth,
- stylistic precision,
- argumentative coherence.
